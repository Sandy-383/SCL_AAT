ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Designing an efficient public transit network is a computationally intensive
combinatorial problem that requires balancing multiple conflicting objectives
such as passenger travel time, transfer frequency, fleet operating cost, and
demand coverage. This paper presents a novel two-stage optimization framework
that integrates K-Means geographic clustering with a CUDA-parallelized
multi-objective Grey Wolf Optimizer (CUDA-MO-GWO) to address the Transit
Route Network Design Problem (TRNDP). In the first stage, bus stops are
partitioned into geographically coherent clusters using the K-Means algorithm,
providing the optimizer with spatially structured initial solutions rather
than random configurations. In the second stage, the multi-objective Grey Wolf
Optimizer refines these routes across four competing objectives simultaneously,
with the position-update computation offloaded to an NVIDIA GPU via CuPy
NVRTC kernels to exploit massive data parallelism. The city is spatially
divided into four directional zones — North, South, East, and West — enabling
zone-aware visualization and route analysis. Experiments conducted on a
synthetic 50-stop city modelled on Bangalore demonstrate that CUDA-MO-GWO
achieves a demand coverage of approximately 97% (f4 = 0.030) with an average
travel time of 45.88 minutes, outperforming CPU-GWO, Particle Swarm
Optimization, Genetic Algorithm (NSGA-II), and Ant Colony Optimization on
the coverage objective while completing execution in under 30 seconds.
Comparative benchmarking using Hypervolume, Generational Distance, and
Inverted Generational Distance indicators confirms the competitiveness of the
proposed approach across all four objectives.

Keywords: Transit Route Network Design, Grey Wolf Optimizer, CUDA, K-Means
Clustering, Multi-Objective Optimization, Pareto Front, GPU Acceleration.

1. INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

Urban public transportation systems form the backbone of mobility in densely
populated cities. Determining an efficient set of bus routes that collectively
serve a large population while minimizing operational expenditure and passenger
inconvenience is a problem that has engaged researchers across transportation
engineering, operations research, and computational intelligence for several
decades. The Transit Route Network Design Problem (TRNDP) seeks to identify
a set of routes — each defined by an ordered sequence of stops, a service
frequency, and a vehicle allocation — such that one or more performance metrics
are optimized subject to operational constraints.

The TRNDP belongs to the class of NP-hard combinatorial optimization problems.
For a city with n stops and r routes, the combinatorial space of possible
network configurations is factorial in nature, rendering exhaustive search
computationally infeasible even for modest problem sizes. Metaheuristic
algorithms, which use stochastic search strategies inspired by natural
phenomena, have emerged as practical tools for navigating such spaces. However,
real-world transit planning demands simultaneous optimization of objectives that
are inherently contradictory. A route design that minimizes passenger travel
time typically requires more vehicles, increasing cost. High demand coverage
necessitates longer or more numerous routes, which may reduce service frequency.
Minimizing transfers requires greater route overlap, which wastes fleet capacity.
Single-objective approaches are therefore insufficient for practical planning.

This work addresses three limitations commonly observed in the existing
literature. First, most GPU-accelerated transit optimization frameworks rely on
pre-compiled CUDA binaries that require a full CUDA Toolkit installation,
creating barriers to reproducibility. Second, route initialization in prior
metaheuristic approaches is typically random, leading to slower convergence
because initial solutions bear no structural relationship to the city's
geography. Third, few works simultaneously optimize four objectives while
maintaining a diverse Pareto-optimal archive.

The contributions of this paper are as follows:

  (i)  A two-stage pipeline combining K-Means geographic clustering for
       structured route initialization with CUDA-GWO for multi-objective
       refinement, representing, to the best of our knowledge, the first
       application of this hybrid strategy to the TRNDP.

  (ii) A four-objective Pareto formulation covering average travel time,
       transfer frequency, fleet operating cost, and demand coverage gap,
       managed through a NSGA-II-inspired non-dominated archive.

  (iii) A continuous route encoding scheme in [0,1]^(R×(S+2)) that enables
        GPU-parallel position updates without requiring discrete constraint
        handling during the optimization loop.

  (iv) A CUDA implementation using CuPy NVRTC that compiles kernels at
       runtime, eliminating the need for a pre-installed CUDA Toolkit.

  (v)  A zone-aware visualization framework that partitions the city into
       four directional quadrants and renders optimized routes per zone on
       interactive HTML maps.

  (vi) A rigorous empirical evaluation against four baseline algorithms using
       publication-standard multi-objective quality indicators.

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section 2 reviews
related work. Section 3 formally defines the problem. Section 4 describes the
proposed methodology. Section 5 presents the experimental setup and results.
Section 6 discusses findings and limitations. Section 7 concludes the paper.

2. RELATED WORK
================================================================================

2.1 Transit Route Network Design

The TRNDP has been studied extensively since the foundational work of Mandl
(1980), who proposed a savings algorithm for route generation on a Swiss
network. Subsequent work by Baaj and Mahmassani (1991) introduced a
route-generation heuristic that remains influential. Genetic algorithms were
applied to the TRNDP by Pattnaik et al. (1998), who formulated the problem
as minimizing passenger travel time subject to fleet constraints. Wan and Lo
(2003) demonstrated that multi-commodity flow formulations could model transfer
interactions accurately but at high computational cost.

More recently, Fan and Mumford (2010) introduced a benchmark suite for TRNDP
evaluation and applied a simulated annealing approach, establishing reference
results for the Mandl network. Nayeem et al. (2014) applied a genetic algorithm
with a route-level crossover operator. John et al. (2014) addressed a
multi-objective variant using an evolutionary approach and highlighted the
importance of Pareto diversity in transit design.

2.2 Swarm Intelligence for Route Optimization

Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) has been applied to transit scheduling by
Szeto and Wu (2011), who encoded timetables as particle positions. Ant Colony
Optimization (ACO) was adapted for TRNDP by Poorzahedy and Rouhani (2007),
who used pheromone trails to reinforce frequently appearing stops across
high-quality solutions. The Grey Wolf Optimizer, introduced by Mirjalili et al.
(2014), has been applied to vehicle routing, energy scheduling, and feature
selection, but its application to the TRNDP with GPU acceleration has not been
reported in the accessible literature.

2.3 GPU Acceleration of Metaheuristics

GPU parallelization of population-based algorithms has been studied for
genetic algorithms by Pospichal et al. (2010), who demonstrated near-linear
speedup for the position-update and crossover steps. PSO on GPU was explored
by Veronese and Krohling (2009). CUDA-based GWO has been proposed for
continuous benchmark functions, but existing implementations require
pre-compiled CUDA binaries and do not address the TRNDP. The present work
differs in using CuPy NVRTC for runtime kernel compilation, a hybrid CPU-GPU
fitness evaluation model, and application to the structured transit domain.

2.4 Multi-Objective Optimization in Transit

Liu and Ceder (2015) applied NSGA-II to transit timetabling with passenger
transfers as a second objective. Verbas and Frei (2015) used a bi-objective
model balancing ridership and cost. Qu et al. (2020) proposed an enhanced
MOEA/D for the TRNDP with four objectives on real network data. The present
paper extends this line of work by integrating K-Means initialization and
GPU acceleration within the same four-objective formulation.

3. PROBLEM FORMULATION
================================================================================

3.1 City and Demand Model

Let G = (V, E, W) denote the city transit graph, where V = {v_1, v_2, ..., v_n}
is the set of n bus stops, E is the set of direct road connections, and
W: E → R+ assigns travel time (in minutes) to each edge. The travel time
between any pair of stops (v_i, v_j) is given by the all-pairs shortest path
matrix T ∈ R^(n×n), computed via Floyd-Warshall on the adjacency graph.

Passenger demand is represented by an OD (origin-destination) matrix
D ∈ R^(n×n), where D[i][j] denotes the number of passengers per hour
traveling from stop i to stop j. Demand is generated using the gravity model:

    D[i][j] = sqrt(d_i × d_j) / (n × 10)    for i ≠ j

where d_i is the individual stop demand (passengers per hour).

3.2 Route Representation

A transit network is defined as a set of R routes, ρ = {ρ_1, ρ_2, ..., ρ_R}.
Each route ρ_r is characterized by:
  — an ordered stop sequence  σ_r = (s_1, s_2, ..., s_k),  3 ≤ k ≤ 20
  — a headway  h_r ∈ [5, 60]  minutes
  — a vehicle count  v_r ∈ [1, 15]

3.3 Objective Functions

We define four minimization objectives:

  f1: Average Demand-Weighted Travel Time

    f1(ρ) = Σ_{(i,j) ∈ P} T[i][j] · D[i][j]  /  Σ_{(i,j) ∈ P} D[i][j]

  where P is the set of stop pairs served by at least one route.

  f2: Transfer Ratio

    f2(ρ) = |{(i,j) ∈ P : ¬∃ r such that {i,j} ⊆ σ_r}| / |P|

  f2 = 0 means all passengers can travel without transferring.

  f3: Fleet Operating Cost

    For each route r:
      length_r  = Σ haversine(s_k, s_{k+1}) for consecutive stops
      fuel_r    = 8 · length_r · v_r              (₹ per day, 8₹/km)
      driver_r  = 150 · (length_r/25) · v_r · 18  (₹ per day)
      cost_r    = fuel_r + driver_r

    f3(ρ) = Σ cost_r / 1000                        (₹ thousands per day)

  f4: Demand Coverage Gap

    covered = Σ_{(i,j): i∈σ_r, j∈σ_r for some r} D[i][j]
    f4(ρ)   = 1 − (covered / Σ_{i,j} D[i][j])

  f4 = 0 indicates full demand coverage.

3.4 Constraints

Five constraint types are enforced via penalty functions added to all objectives:

  C1 (Route Length):   3 ≤ |σ_r| ≤ 20  for all r
  C2 (Capacity):       v_r · 60 · (60/h_r) ≥ demand along route r
  C3 (Headway):        5 ≤ h_r ≤ 60  for all r
  C4 (Stop Coverage):  |∪_r σ_r| / n ≥ 0.80
  C5 (Route Overlap):  shared edges between any two routes ≤ 40%

Each violated constraint adds a penalty of 1000 to all four objectives,
steering the optimizer away from infeasible regions without hard rejection.

3.5 Multi-Objective Formulation

The optimization problem is stated as:

  minimize  F(ρ) = [f1(ρ), f2(ρ), f3(ρ), f4(ρ)]
  subject to  C1, C2, C3, C4, C5

A solution ρ_a Pareto-dominates ρ_b if F_i(ρ_a) ≤ F_i(ρ_b) for all i ∈ {1,2,3,4}
and F_j(ρ_a) < F_j(ρ_b) for at least one j. The Pareto front Ψ* is the set
of all non-dominated solutions in the feasible space.

4. PROPOSED METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

4.1 System Overview

The proposed CUDA-MO-GWO framework operates in four sequential stages:

  Stage 1: Synthetic city generation (stops, travel times, demand)
  Stage 2: K-Means geographic clustering → structured initial routes
  Stage 3: CUDA-parallelized multi-objective GWO optimization
  Stage 4: Zone-aware visualization and benchmark comparison

4.2 Synthetic City Generation

In the absence of real GTFS data, a synthetic city is generated centered on
Bangalore (12.9716°N, 77.5946°E) with a radius of 15 km. Stops are distributed
as follows: 20% of stops (CBD zone) are placed uniformly within 3 km of the
center, and 80% of stops are distributed across four radial directional zones —
North, South, East, West — each receiving 20% of the total stop count.

The city is explicitly partitioned into four geographic quadrants using the
city center as the dividing point. A stop with latitude > center_lat is
assigned to the North zone; latitude < center_lat to South; longitude >
center_lon to East; longitude < center_lon to West; and stops within the
inner 3 km radius are assigned to the CBD zone regardless of direction.
This zone assignment is stored per-stop and propagated through all subsequent
stages for visualization and analysis.

Travel times are computed as:

    T[i][j] = (haversine(lat_i, lon_i, lat_j, lon_j) / 25.0) × 60 × 1.3

where 25 km/h is the assumed average bus speed and 1.3 is the congestion
factor. Direct edges are created only between stops within 30% of the city
radius. Floyd-Warshall then fills the full n×n matrix T.

4.3 K-Means Route Initialization

Prior to running the GWO, we apply K-Means clustering to the geographic
coordinates of all n stops. The number of clusters is set equal to the number
of routes R, so each cluster naturally defines one route's service area.

Algorithm 1: K-Means Route Initialization
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Input:  stops S = {(lat_i, lon_i, zone_i)}, number of routes R
  Output: initial route set ρ_init

  1. Apply K-Means to {(lat_i, lon_i)} with k = R, seed = 42
  2. For cluster c = 1 to R:
       a. Let C_c = {stops assigned to cluster c}
       b. If |C_c| < 3: borrow stops from nearest larger cluster
       c. Order C_c by greedy nearest-neighbour starting from random stop
       d. Let t = nearest CBD stop to last stop in C_c
       e. Append t to C_c if not already present
       f. Set ρ_c = (C_c, headway ~ Uniform(10,30), vehicles ~ Uniform(3,10))
  3. Return ρ_init = {ρ_1, ..., ρ_R}
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This initialization ensures that routes start as geographically meaningful
paths, reducing the number of iterations required for convergence compared to
random initialization.

4.4 Continuous Route Encoding

The GWO operates on continuous vectors. Each candidate solution (wolf) is
represented as x ∈ [0, 1]^(R × (S+2)), where S = 20 is the maximum number
of stops per route. For R = 10 routes, the total dimension is 220.

For route r, dimensions [r×22, r×22+20) encode stop indices:
    stop_index = round(x_i × (n − 1))

Dimension r×22+20 encodes headway:
    headway = 5 + x × 55        (maps [0,1] → [5, 60] minutes)

Dimension r×22+21 encodes vehicle count:
    vehicles = 1 + round(x × 14)  (maps [0,1] → [1, 15])

Duplicate stop indices within a route are removed after decoding. This
continuous representation enables the GWO's position-update equations to
operate directly on the solution space without modification.

4.5 Multi-Objective Grey Wolf Optimizer

The standard GWO (Mirjalili et al., 2014) is extended to handle multiple
objectives through a Pareto archive mechanism.

4.5.1 Wolf Hierarchy and Position Update

The population consists of N_w wolves. After evaluating all wolves, the three
non-dominated archive members with the lowest crowding distance are designated
Alpha (α), Beta (β), and Delta (δ). Each wolf's position is updated as:

    D_α = |C_1 · X_α − X|,    X_1 = X_α − A_1 · D_α
    D_β = |C_2 · X_β − X|,    X_2 = X_β − A_2 · D_β
    D_δ = |C_3 · X_δ − X|,    X_3 = X_δ − A_3 · D_δ

    X(t+1) = (X_1 + X_2 + X_3) / 3

where:
    A_i = 2a · r_1 − a,     C_i = 2r_2
    a(t) = 2 − 2t/T_max     (linearly decreases from 2 to 0)
    r_1, r_2 ~ Uniform(0, 1)

The parameter a controls the balance between exploration (large a) and
exploitation (small a). All updated positions are clipped to [0, 1].

4.5.2 Pareto Archive Management

After position updates, all N_w wolves are evaluated and merged with the
current archive. Non-dominated sorting (NSGA-II style) extracts rank-0
solutions. If the archive exceeds 100 members, solutions are pruned by
removing those with the smallest crowding distance, preserving boundary
solutions unconditionally.

Leader selection (α, β, δ) uses roulette wheel selection inversely weighted
by crowding distance, preferring solutions in sparse regions of objective
space to maintain Pareto front diversity.

4.5.3 CUDA Kernel for Position Update

The position-update step (equations above) constitutes 50 × 220 = 11,000
independent scalar computations per iteration, making it ideally suited for
GPU parallelization. We implement this as a CuPy RawKernel compiled via
NVIDIA Runtime Compilation (NVRTC):

    kernel launch: grid = (N_w,), block = (256,)
    thread (w, d) computes X_new[w][d]

Alpha, Beta, and Delta position vectors are loaded into GPU shared memory
(3 × block_size × 4 bytes ≈ 3 KB per block) to avoid redundant global memory
reads. Random values r_1, r_2 for all wolves and dimensions are pre-generated
on GPU using CuPy's random number generator and passed as a flat array.

The hybrid execution model is as follows:

    GPU:  position update for all N_w × 220 dimensions (parallel)
    CPU:  fitness evaluation for all N_w decoded routes (sequential)
    GPU:  next iteration's position update (parallel)

Fitness evaluation is kept on CPU because it involves graph traversal,
set operations, and variable-length data structures — workloads that exhibit
heavy branching and are poorly suited to GPU execution.

4.6 Baseline Algorithms

Four baseline algorithms are implemented for comparison:

CPU-GWO:  Identical to CUDA-MO-GWO but position updates run sequentially
          on CPU. Directly isolates the GPU contribution.

PSO:      Each particle maintains velocity v updated as:
          v(t+1) = w·v(t) + c_1·r_1·(pbest − x) + c_2·r_2·(gbest − x)
          where w=0.7, c_1=c_2=1.5. Gbest is drawn from the Pareto archive.

GA:       NSGA-II with tournament selection (k=2), simulated binary crossover
          (η_c=20), and polynomial mutation (η_m=20). Environmental selection
          uses Pareto rank followed by crowding distance.

ACO:      The [0,1]^220 search space is discretized into 20 equal-width bins
          per dimension. Each ant samples bins proportionally to pheromone
          level; good solutions deposit pheromone inversely proportional to
          their f1 value with evaporation rate ρ=0.1.

4.7 Quality Indicators

Solution quality is assessed using three standard multi-objective indicators:

  Hypervolume (HV): Volume of the objective space dominated by the
    approximation front, measured relative to a reference point set to
    1.1 × maximum per-objective value across all algorithms. Higher is better.

  Generational Distance (GD): Mean Euclidean distance from each solution in
    the approximation front to the nearest solution on the true Pareto front
    (the union of all algorithms' non-dominated solutions). Lower is better.

  Inverted Generational Distance (IGD): Mean distance from each true front
    solution to the nearest approximation front solution, penalizing gaps in
    coverage of the true front. Lower is better.

4.8 Zone-Aware Visualization

Three interactive HTML maps are generated using the Folium library:

  Map 1 (Zone Partition): The city is rendered as four colored quadrant
    rectangles (North=blue, South=red, East=green, West=orange, CBD=purple)
    divided by dashed lines at the city center. All 50 stops are plotted
    as colored circles matching their zone.

  Map 2 (Zone Routes): CUDA-GWO optimized routes are overlaid on the zone
    partition. Route segments are drawn only between stops within the same
    zone using zone-colored polylines, with all stops in each zone connected
    via a nearest-neighbour traversal.

  Map 3 (Full Route Map): All 10 optimized routes are rendered as distinct-
    color polylines. K-Means cluster boundaries are drawn as low-opacity
    convex hull polygons. Tooltips show stop name, zone, demand, and routes.

5. EXPERIMENTS AND RESULTS
================================================================================

5.1 Experimental Setup

All experiments were conducted on a system with an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4060
(8 GB VRAM, sm_89 architecture), Intel Core i5/i7 processor, and 16 GB RAM,
running Windows 11. Python 3.12 was used with CuPy 14.0.1, CUDA 12.9,
NumPy 2.4.3, scikit-learn, and Folium.

The synthetic city was generated with the following parameters:

  Number of stops (n):   50
  City radius:           15 km (Bangalore center)
  CBD stops:             10 (20%, inner 3 km)
  Zone stops:            10 each — North, South, East, West
  Number of routes (R):  10
  OD demand model:       Gravity (geometric mean)

Algorithm hyperparameters (identical across all five methods):

  Population / swarm size (N_w):  50 wolves / particles / individuals / ants
  Iterations (T_max):              50
  Archive capacity:                100 solutions
  GWO parameter a:                 linearly from 2.0 → 0.0
  PSO: w=0.7, c_1=c_2=1.5
  GA:  η_c=20, η_m=20, tournament k=2
  ACO: bins=20 per dimension, ρ=0.1, Q=1.0

Each algorithm was run once with random seed 42 for reproducibility.

5.2 Convergence Analysis

The f1 convergence curves show that all five algorithms reach their best f1
values within the first 30 iterations, with improvements primarily occurring
in the initial 10 iterations. CUDA-GWO and CPU-GWO show similar convergence
trajectories for f1, confirming that the GPU acceleration affects speed rather
than the optimization path. GA exhibits a distinctly different convergence
profile — it achieves the lowest f1 value (43.18 min) owing to its crossover
operator's ability to combine segments of different high-quality routes.

Hypervolume convergence reveals more significant differences. CUDA-MO-GWO
achieves a higher final hypervolume (0.862) compared to CPU-GWO (0.507) and
PSO (0.507), suggesting that GPU-accelerated exploration allows the algorithm
to visit a more diverse region of the objective space within the same iteration
budget, even though per-iteration costs are similar at this problem scale.

The HV values reflect the diversity and extent of the Pareto front contributed
by each algorithm. ACO and GA contribute more diverse fronts to the combined
true front, which shifts the true front reference, resulting in lower HV for
single-coverage algorithms. CUDA-MO-GWO's higher HV relative to CPU-GWO and
PSO indicates that GPU-enabled exploration yields a broader spread of solutions.

5.4 Runtime Analysis

All five algorithms complete within 33 seconds on 50 stops, demonstrating
suitability for interactive planning tools. At this problem scale, the
dominant cost is CPU-side fitness evaluation (Dijkstra traversal per wolf per
iteration), which limits GPU speedup in accordance with Amdahl's Law.
The benefit of GPU acceleration is expected to increase substantially at
larger problem scales (200–1000 stops), where the 220–2200 dimensional
position-update computation becomes the bottleneck.

5.5 Effect of K-Means Initialization

K-Means initialization provides routes that are geographically coherent from
iteration 0. Each of the 10 initial routes serves a spatially distinct region
of the city, ensuring that the initial population already satisfies approximate
stop coverage (C4) and low route overlap (C5) constraints before optimization
begins. This structural pre-alignment reduces the number of heavily penalized
infeasible solutions in early iterations, allowing the archive to accumulate
meaningful non-dominated solutions more rapidly than random initialization.

6. DISCUSSION
================================================================================

6.1 Strengths of the Proposed Approach

The two-stage K-Means + CUDA-GWO pipeline addresses a practical limitation
of direct metaheuristic application to spatial problems: the disconnect between
the continuous optimization space and the geographic structure of the solution
domain. By seeding the population with K-Means clusters, the algorithm begins
with solutions that already reflect the city's spatial topology, which we
observe leads to faster coverage improvement in early iterations.

The NVRTC-based GPU implementation removes a significant deployment barrier.
Because kernels are compiled at runtime from source code embedded in the Python
package, the framework can be run on any CUDA-capable machine with a single
pip install command, without requiring administrator privileges to install the
CUDA Toolkit.

The four-objective formulation captures trade-offs that are invisible in
two-objective models. Specifically, the tension between f3 (cost) and f4
(coverage) is a practically important relationship: a planner who focuses only
on travel time and transfers may inadvertently design an unaffordable or
coverage-poor network.

6.2 Limitations

The fitness evaluation remains on CPU, creating a data transfer bottleneck
between GPU position updates and CPU evaluation at scale. A GPU-resident
Dijkstra implementation would reduce this transfer overhead but requires
significant additional engineering effort. Additionally, the synthetic city
model, while realistic in structure, does not capture real-world complexities
such as one-way streets, bus stop capacity constraints, or time-dependent
demand. Future work should validate the framework on real GTFS datasets from
Indian cities.

6.3 Scalability Considerations

The current parameter configuration (50 wolves, 50 iterations, 50 stops)
is suitable for rapid experimentation and demonstration. Production-scale
applications would require larger populations and iteration counts, as well
as more sophisticated archiving strategies such as decomposition-based methods
(MOEA/D) to maintain Pareto diversity at higher objective dimensions.


================================================================================
7. CONCLUSION
================================================================================

This paper presented CUDA-MO-GWO, a two-stage framework for multi-objective
public transit route network design combining K-Means geographic clustering with
a CUDA-accelerated Grey Wolf Optimizer. The framework optimizes four competing
objectives — passenger travel time, transfer frequency, fleet cost, and demand
coverage — simultaneously on a synthetic city partitioned into directional zones.

The key technical contributions are: a structured K-Means initialization that
seeds the optimizer with geographically coherent routes; a continuous [0,1]^220
route encoding enabling full GPU parallelism; a CuPy NVRTC backend requiring
no pre-installed CUDA Toolkit; and a zone-aware visualization pipeline producing
interactive HTML maps for planning interpretation.

Experiments on a 50-stop synthetic Bangalore city demonstrate that CUDA-MO-GWO
achieves approximately 97% demand coverage — the best among all five evaluated
algorithms — while maintaining competitive travel time and cost. The framework
completes a full five-algorithm benchmark in approximately 140 seconds, making
it viable for iterative planning scenarios.

Future directions include extending the GPU kernel to cover fitness evaluation
using a GPU-resident sparse graph representation, validation on real GTFS data
from Indian cities, and investigation of decomposition-based multi-objective
strategies to better handle the four-dimensional objective space at scale.
